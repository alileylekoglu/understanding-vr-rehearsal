01 — INTRODUCTION

Why VR for amateur theatre?

INTRODUCTION

Theatre rehearsal requires actors to explore space, timing, and interaction while staying connected with scene partners. When access to physical spaces is limited, virtual reality (VR) can support embodied rehearsal, such as blocking and social relationships. However, effective VR rehearsal depends not only on visual realism but also on presence and credible interaction, including gesture, gaze, and turn-taking. Current systems still limit expressive embodiment and may cause discomfort, so design must balance usability and rehearsal needs.

MAIN CONTRIBUTION

This study focuses on amateur theatre, which is common but underexplored in VR rehearsal research. Using a forced-ranking task and thematic analysis, we examine how amateur actors prioritize VR rehearsal support themes and why. The results, based on Borda Count and codebook thematic analysis, provide clear priorities and practical insights for designing actor-centered VR rehearsal systems.

RESEARCH QUESTIONS
RQ1

How are six VR rehearsal support themes prioritized?

RQ2

What rationales and expectations are expressed for these priorities?

02 — METHOD

How we studied it

RECRUITMENT

N = 40 amateur actors were recruited from Özyeğin University through a university-affiliated theatre community. Participation was voluntary.

THEME REFINEMENT

Six themes based on a prior study with amateur actors (Leylekoğlu & Baykal, 2024), labels and descriptions were revised using relevant literature (Levordashka et al. 2023; McKendrick et al. 2023; Wales et al. 2024; Leylekoğlu & Baykal, 2025) to make them clearer, reduce overlap, and ensure they were suitable for amateur actors without prior VR experience.

02 — METHOD

Six VR Rehearsal Support Themes

01
Social Interaction & Co-presence
Supporting responsive rehearsal interaction with others in VR.
Multi-actor co-presence; NPC partners; asynchronous audio recordings
02
Stage Layout, Set & Props
Supporting blocking and staging through virtual set and prop representations.
Placement of decorations; prop representations; stage layout simulation
03
Script & Directorial Guidance
Supporting rehearsal through script interaction and director-led guidance.
In-script note-taking; highlighting; director annotations; personalized layout
04
Rehearsal Logistics & Continuity
Reducing disruptions and supporting continuity across rehearsal sessions.
Supporting rehearsals despite space constraints; facilitating coordination; maintaining rehearsal flow
05
Comfort & Spatial Usability
Configuring a comfortable and usable VR rehearsal space.
Adjustable lighting; sufficient movement space; visual comfort
06
Practice & Feedback Tools
Supporting individual practice, memorization, and self-improvement.
Repetition support; memory games; drawing/writing tools; self-review recordings
02 — METHOD

Data Collection & Analysis

DATA COLLECTION
Web-based Survey

Demographic questions, a forced-ranking task of the six VR rehearsal support themes (1 – most, 6 – least; no duplicate ranks), and open-ended questions about their choices.

QuantitativeQualitative
ANALYSIS Quant
Borda Count Aggregation

Rank positions assigned fixed weights (1st=6, 2nd=5, 3rd=4, 4th=3, 5th=2, 6th=1). For each theme, a total Borda score was computed by summing these weights across all participants (N=40). Themes were ordered from highest to lowest score.

ANALYSIS Qual
Codebook Thematic Analysis

A deductive approach was used, with the six predefined rehearsal support themes serving as the initial coding framework. Responses were coded at the semantic level by a single researcher following a predefined codebook with inclusion and exclusion criteria.

04 — PRELIMINARY RESULTS

3.2 Codebook Thematic Analysis

#1 Social Interaction & Co-presence
Actors value interaction as essential for rehearsal. Communication, shared scene-building, and responsiveness are key. VR should support co-presence rather than individual practice.
"In a team without communication, the chances of producing a strong and successful play are low."P39
#2 Stage Layout, Set & Props
Early access to stage elements helps actors understand space, blocking, and positioning. In traditional rehearsals, sets come late, making adaptation difficult.
"Since the set of a play usually takes its final form only in the later stages of rehearsals, it gives the actor very little time to adapt."P22
#3 Script & Directorial Guidance
Script and director input help structure rehearsal and support character development. They also support continuity during rehearsal.
"I see script and dramaturgy as important elements in managing the play process and character creation."P3
04 — PRELIMINARY RESULTS

3.2 Codebook Thematic Analysis (continued)

#4 Rehearsal Logistics & Continuity
Scheduling, space, and coordination problems reduce rehearsal efficiency. VR can help address these issues and support continuity.
"VR can make rehearsals that are difficult, time-consuming, and costly more quickly and with fewer difficulties."P12
#5 Comfort & Spatial Usability
A comfortable and usable environment is necessary for a smooth rehearsal process. Poor conditions can affect other aspects of rehearsal.
"I think that if the environment setting is not suitable, the other aspects will also be disrupted."P28
#6 Practice & Feedback Tools
These tools support memorization, repetition, and individual improvement. They allow actors to rehearse independently.
"Being able to rehearse the scene over and over without tiring anyone else besides the actor could be very beneficial."P18
05 — DISCUSSION

Discussion

Interaction over simulation

Amateur actors see VR rehearsal as an interaction-driven activity rather than a visual simulation. Rehearsal value depends on timing, attention, and partner interaction, not visual realism.

Early spatial access matters

Stage Layout is seen as an early rehearsal need. VR helps actors understand space earlier, unlike traditional rehearsals where the set comes late.

Amateur constraints shape priorities

Script and Logistics support rehearsal flow. They help actors continue rehearsals despite limited time, space, and coordination issues.

Cost as a barrier

VR headset cost is a challenge for amateur groups with limited budgets, especially for group rehearsals.

Enabling conditions

Comfort and Practice Tools support the process. They become important when missing or during long use (e.g., cybersickness).

06 — CONCLUSION

Conclusion & Future Work

Amateur actors prioritize supports that enable rehearsal as a shared and embodied process. Co-presence and spatial staging are valued first; script guidance, continuity, and comfort features stabilize the rehearsal workflow over time. These findings suggest actor-centered VR value emerges from interaction plausibility and partner-oriented action rather than representational realism.

Validate priorities through hands-on VR rehearsal sessions. Examine how prioritization shifts across rehearsal phases and production contexts.
